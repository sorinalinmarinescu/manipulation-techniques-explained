# Episode 71 — CASE FILE #9: The Astroturf Campaign
### Toxic political style #2 — a "grassroots movement" that's really a corporate front

**The Case Files** · **Type:** Two-part worked example · **Target runtime:** up to 30 min
**Logline:** A "spontaneous citizens' movement" rises up to fight a new regulation — and turns out to be funded, top-down, by the very industry the rule would cost. **Part 1** follows a composite fight over a safety regulation. **Part 2** breaks down the astroturf playbook: front groups, manufactured doubt, "plain folks," and faked grassroots — and the one defense that beats it all: follow the money.

> **Strictly non-partisan, composite dramatization.** Astroturfing is used by corporations, ideological groups, and political actors of every stripe, for and against all manner of causes. "SafeKids Act," "Americans for Common-Sense Choice," and "MegaCorp" are inventions illustrating a documented pattern.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define astroturfing and recognize a manufactured "grassroots" movement.
- See how front groups + manufactured doubt + "plain folks" combine.
- Make "cui bono — follow the money" your reflex.

---

## COLD OPEN

[B-ROLL: heartfelt "ordinary citizen" ads — "I'm just a mom who believes in freedom of choice. Don't let the government take ours away." A wholesome logo: "Americans for Common-Sense Choice."]

**VO:** A new safety regulation is proposed — let's say it would require a popular product to carry a warning and meet a safety standard. Suddenly, a passionate citizens' movement erupts against it. Heartfelt ads feature ordinary moms and small-business owners: "I'm just a regular person, and I don't want big government taking away my freedom of choice." A grassroots groundswell, the people rising up. Lawmakers get flooded with letters. The regulation stalls.

[Beat.]

**VO:** Except it wasn't grassroots at all. Every bit of it — the group, the ads, the "ordinary citizens," the flood of letters — was conceived, funded, and orchestrated by the industry that would lose money under the rule. It's fake grass. There's a word for that, coined by a U.S. senator who could tell the difference: astroturf. Let's dig it up.

# PART ONE — THE CAMPAIGN UNFOLDS

## STAGE 1 — The threat to profit

[B-ROLL: a corporate boardroom; a proposed regulation that would cut into a product's sales.]

**VO:** It starts in a boardroom. A company — call it MegaCorp — faces a regulation that would cost it money. They could argue against it openly: "this rule will hurt our profits." But "it's bad for our bottom line" doesn't move the public. They need the *public* to fight this for them — to make it look like the people, not the corporation, oppose the rule. So they hire a PR firm with a specialty: manufacturing grassroots.

## STAGE 2 — Building the front

[B-ROLL: the PR firm creating "Americans for Common-Sense Choice" — a warm logo, a website, a mission statement about "freedom," no mention of MegaCorp.]

**VO:** The firm creates a citizens' group with a wonderful, vague, all-American name — "Americans for Common-Sense Choice." It has a logo, a slick website, a mission statement full of words like *freedom*, *choice*, and *common sense.* What it doesn't have, anywhere you can find it, is MegaCorp's name. To the public, it looks like a spontaneous coalition of concerned citizens. In reality, it's a wholly-owned costume.

## STAGE 3 — Manufacturing doubt

[B-ROLL: paid "independent experts" questioning the safety science; "the studies are inconclusive," "more research is needed."]

**VO:** The regulation rests on safety science. So the front group funds a handful of credentialed-sounding "independent experts" to muddy it: "the science isn't settled," "the studies are flawed," "more research is needed before we rush to regulate." They don't need to *win* the science — they just need the public to feel it's a genuine, unresolved controversy, so action stalls. The doubt is the product.

## STAGE 4 — The "plain folks" and the fake flood

[B-ROLL: relatable "ordinary citizen" testimonial ads; thousands of near-identical letters and comments to lawmakers; bot-amplified hashtags.]

**VO:** Now the human face. The campaign features carefully cast "ordinary people" — a mom, a veteran, a small-business owner — speaking from the heart against the rule, never disclosing they're part of a corporate operation. And it generates a *flood*: pre-written letters and comments that supporters just sign and send, so lawmakers' inboxes overflow with "spontaneous" public opposition. Online, coordinated accounts and bots make a hashtag trend. To a legislator, it looks like a tidal wave of constituent anger. To the public, it looks like everyone's against the rule.

## STAGE 5 — The win

[B-ROLL: lawmakers backing off; the regulation watered down or killed; MegaCorp's name never appearing.]

**VO:** It works. Facing what looks like a furious public, lawmakers water down or quietly kill the regulation. MegaCorp gets exactly the outcome it wanted — and its fingerprints are nowhere. The public believes "the people" won a victory against "big government." In fact, a corporation just used the public as a sock puppet against the public's own safety.

# PART TWO — THE BREAKDOWN

[GFX: section header — "HOW IT WAS DONE."]

**VO:** Astroturfing is a combination move. Let's name the parts.

## 1. The front group (third-party technique / astroturf)

**VO:** The core is the **front group** and the **third-party technique** (Episode 6): hide the interested seller behind a "neutral," citizen-sounding organization, because we trust messages more when they don't seem to come from someone with a stake. The word **"astroturf"** — fake grassroots — is credited to U.S. Senator Lloyd Bentsen in 1985, who said he could tell real "grass roots" from "AstroTurf" after being buried in suspiciously identical industry-organized letters. The whole campaign is built to dodge the question you should always ask: *who's really behind this?*

[LOWER THIRD: "Astroturf" (fake grassroots) — coined by U.S. Sen. Lloyd Bentsen, 1985]

## 2. Manufactured doubt

**VO:** Funding "independent experts" to muddy settled science is **manufactured doubt** (Episode 46) — the exact playbook documented in *Merchants of Doubt*, where the same tactics (and sometimes the same people) were used to question the science on tobacco and other public-health issues. The goal isn't to win the argument; it's to manufacture enough "controversy" that action stalls. Doubt is the product, and delay is the victory.

## 3. Plain folks

**VO:** The relatable "I'm just a regular mom" spokespeople are **plain folks** (Episode 45): a powerful interest disguising itself as ordinary citizens to borrow the trust we extend to "people like us." The mom is real; what's hidden is who wrote her script and signs the checks.

## 4. Glittering generalities

**VO:** "Freedom," "choice," "common sense" — **glittering generalities** (Episode 7). Notice the group is never "Americans for MegaCorp's Profits." It's draped in words no one can oppose, so the corporate interest hides inside a value you already hold.

## 5. Bots, fake floods & manufactured consensus

**VO:** The pre-written letter flood and the bot-amplified hashtags are **sockpuppets and manufactured crowds** (Episode 14) exploiting **social proof / bandwagon** (Episode 4): make it *look* like overwhelming public opinion, and both lawmakers and real citizens assume the consensus is genuine and fall in line. A manufactured majority bullies the real, quiet one.

## 6. Why it's so effective — and so corrosive

**VO:** Astroturfing works because it dodges your single best defense against a corporate pitch: the salesperson discount. You *know* to be skeptical when MegaCorp says "don't regulate us." But when "concerned citizens" and "independent experts" say it, your guard drops. The corrosive part is what it does to trust itself: once astroturf is everywhere, people start to suspect that *every* genuine grassroots movement is fake — which conveniently neuters real citizen activism too. Manufactured fake participation poisons the well for the real thing.

**VO:** And the honest caveat: not every citizens' group is a front, and "that's astroturf!" can itself be a smear used to dismiss authentic, inconvenient public movements. Real grassroots movements exist and matter. The skill, again, isn't blanket cynicism — it's the specific habit of checking *who funds the messenger.*

## THE TELLS — and the defense

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Astroturf Campaigns]
- **A sudden, well-funded "grassroots" movement** that happens to align perfectly with an industry's or interest's profits.
- **Vague, virtuous name; hidden funders.** "Americans for [Glittering Generality]" with no clear "who pays for this?" page.
- **Manufactured doubt** from "independent experts" who muddy settled science.
- **Suspiciously uniform "spontaneous" support** — identical letters, comments, talking points; bot-driven trends.
- **"Plain folks"** spokespeople whose backing is undisclosed.

**VO:** The defense is two words you now know by heart: **follow the money.** For any "citizens' coalition," "institute," or "movement," spend five minutes finding its funders — search the name plus "funding" or "donors." A genuine grassroots group is usually proud to tell you who backs it; a front group buries it. And ask the eternal question — **cui bono?** If this campaign succeeds, who profits? When the answer is "a specific industry," and that industry is nowhere on the label, you've found the astroturf.

## TRY IT YOURSELF

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, pick one advocacy group or "coalition" you've seen in the news — ideally one whose message you *agree* with, to keep yourself honest — and spend five minutes finding out who funds it. Sometimes you'll find an open, genuine nonprofit. Sometimes you'll find a single industry writing the checks behind a wholesome name. Either way, you've built the one reflex that defeats astroturf: never judge a message without asking who paid for the messenger.

## RECAP

**VO:** The astroturf campaign fakes a whole grassroots movement to fight for a hidden interest: a vaguely-named front group, manufactured doubt from "independent" experts, "plain folks" spokespeople, and a bot-amplified flood of fake "spontaneous" support — all to dodge your skepticism of the real, profit-driven sponsor. The defense is eternal: follow the money, and ask cui bono.
