# Episode 48 — Paltering
### How to mislead with nothing but true statements

**Module 6 · Episode 48** · **Target runtime:** 11–12 min
**Anchor callback:** EP44 (card stacking) — its sharper, sentence-level cousin.
**Logline:** Paltering is deceiving someone using statements that are technically true. You never tell a lie you could be caught on — you just arrange true facts to create a false impression. It's the favorite trick of anyone who wants deniability.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define paltering and distinguish it from outright lying and from lying by omission.
- Understand why "technically true" gives manipulators deniability.
- Ask about the *impression*, not just the literal words.

---

## COLD OPEN

[B-ROLL: a used-car seller: "This car? The engine was completely rebuilt last year." (True. He doesn't mention it was rebuilt because it failed catastrophically and may fail again.)]

**VO:** "This car's engine was completely rebuilt just last year." Every word is true. What the seller doesn't say is *why* it was rebuilt — because it blew up — and that it might do so again. He didn't lie. He told you a true fact, chosen precisely to make you believe something false. If you call him on it later, he'll shrug: "I never said anything untrue."

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Paltering:** actively creating a false impression using statements that are technically true.

**VO:** Researchers who study negotiation gave this old trick a precise name: paltering. It sits between two other deceptions. **Lying** is stating something false. **Lying by omission** is staying silent about something important. Paltering is the sly middle: *actively saying true things* in order to deceive. It's the politician's favorite, the salesperson's escape hatch, the cheater's alibi — because every individual statement can pass a fact-check.

**VO:** The classic real-world example: a leader asked about a scandal who answers a slightly different question truthfully, technically responsive, deliberately misleading. "I did not authorize *that specific document*" — true, while a dozen others were authorized. The literal words are a fortress; the impression is a lie.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works (and why manipulators love it)

[GFX: a statement that's "100% true" on the surface, with a false impression growing underneath it.]

**VO:** Paltering works because of how we listen. We don't store the exact literal words; we extract the *gist* — the overall impression — and remember that. So a speaker can craft true statements engineered to produce a false gist, knowing you'll walk away believing the false version while they keep a true-statements alibi.

**VO:** And that alibi is the whole appeal. Outright lies are risky — they can be exposed, and they carry moral and legal weight. Paltering offers deniability: "I never said that. Check the transcript." Interestingly, research finds that people who palter think it's more ethical than outright lying — but the people *deceived* by it feel just as betrayed once they realize. The deceiver gets to feel honest while doing the work of a liar. That self-justification is part of why it's so common.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the wild

[B-ROLL: montage — a label "made with real fruit"; a politician's carefully true non-answer; "no added sugar" on a sugary product.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** "Made with real fruit juice" (a tiny percentage). "No artificial colors" (loaded with artificial flavors and sugar instead). "Up to 70% off" (one item is, the rest aren't). Each is true; each is built to mislead. The label is a paltering machine.

**VO:** **Politics and PR.** The carefully-true non-answer; citing a real statistic that's accurate but irrelevant or cherry-picked; "mistakes were made" (true, and conveniently agentless). When a spokesperson answers a question you didn't ask — truthfully — watch out: you're likely being paltered.

**VO:** **Everyday life.** "I was at the library" (for ten minutes, on the way somewhere they're hiding). The cheating partner, the evasive employee, the seller of a flawed thing — paltering is the deception of choice for anyone who wants to be able to say, with a clear face, "I never lied to you."

---

## SEGMENT 4 — Listening for the impression

[GFX: a person mentally asking, "what does he want me to believe — and is THAT true?"]

**VO:** The defense is to stop evaluating only the literal words and start evaluating the *impression* they're designed to create — then test whether *that* is true.

[ON SCREEN — THE PALTERING DEFENSE:]
- **Ask: "What do they want me to conclude?"** Then ask: "Is *that conclusion* actually true?" Paltering survives a check of the words but fails a check of the impression.
- **Answer or dodge?** If someone truthfully answers a question slightly different from the one asked, re-ask the exact question. Pin it down.
- **Probe for the omission.** "Is there anything about this that would change my mind if I knew it?" Paltering depends on what's left unsaid around the true statement.
- **Watch for the alibi structure.** Language carefully built so it can later be defended word-for-word is a tell that the impression and the literal truth were meant to diverge.

**VO:** The mindset shift: a statement can be 100% true and 100% deceptive at the same time. So "is that true?" is the wrong question. The right one is "what are they trying to make me believe, and is *that* true?"

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Paltering]
- **True words, false feeling.** You verified the statement, yet the overall impression is off — classic paltering.
- **Answered a different question.** A truthful response to a question you didn't ask is a dodge.
- **The convenient omission.** A true fact that only misleads because of what's left out around it.
- **Pre-built deniability.** Phrasing suspiciously easy to defend "literally" later.

**VO:** The one-liner: *check the impression, not just the words — a true sentence can carry a false message.*

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, catch one "technically true" claim — a food label, an ad, a politician's answer — and separate two things: what was literally said, and what you were *meant to conclude*. Then test the conclusion. "Made with real fruit" → meant to conclude "this is healthy/fruity" → is *that* true? Doing this a few times trains you to hear the message under the words.

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Paltering deceives with technically-true statements, giving the manipulator deniability while you walk away believing something false. The defense: judge the impression they're building, not just the literal words — and ask whether *that* impression is true.
