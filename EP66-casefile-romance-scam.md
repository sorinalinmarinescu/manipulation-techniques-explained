# Episode 66 — CASE FILE #4: The Romance Scam
### When the manipulator makes you fall in love

**The Case Files** · **Type:** Two-part worked example · **Target runtime:** up to 30 min
**Logline:** The cruelest scam weaponizes the strongest human force: love. **Part 1** follows a composite victim, Linda, from a friendly message to a hundred-thousand-dollar loss and a broken heart. **Part 2** breaks down how romance scammers combine love bombing, isolation, intermittent reinforcement, sunk cost, and now AI voice-cloning — and why intelligence is no protection.

> **Composite dramatization** built from documented romance-scam and "pig-butchering" patterns. "David," Linda, and the details are inventions. The harm is real and massive: U.S. consumers reported about **$1.3 billion** lost to romance scams in **2022** (FTC), roughly **70,000** reports, with a median individual loss in the thousands — and far higher for crypto-investment-romance hybrids.

**Learning objectives:**
- See how scammers manufacture real love and exploit it for money.
- Map the romance-scam arc to relationship-manipulation techniques.
- Learn the verification habits (and the compassion) that defeat it.

He's attentive, funny, a wonderful listener. Within weeks she's in love. Within months she's sent him over a hundred thousand dollars. David does not exist. He never did.

Because this scam doesn't attack your intelligence. It attacks your *heart* — and the heart has no firewall. Let's see how it's done, with compassion, because the goal is to protect the people we love, including ourselves.

# PART ONE — THE CAMPAIGN UNFOLDS

## STAGE 1 — The approach & the love bomb

[B-ROLL: a too-perfect profile; a flood of warm, attentive messages; rapid intimacy.]

**VO:** David's profile is attractive but believable. His first messages are warm and curious — all about *her*. He's an extraordinary listener, remembers every detail, texts good morning and good night. He seems to share all her values, her sense of humor, her grief (he's a widower too). The intimacy escalates fast: within weeks he's calling her his soulmate, saying he's never felt this way. This is **love bombing** — and it lands hardest on someone lonely, who has been starved of exactly this attention.

## STAGE 2 — Building trust & isolation

[B-ROLL: long daily conversations; David subtly discouraging Linda from sharing the relationship.]

**VO:** They talk for hours every day. David becomes the center of Linda's emotional life. But he's also, gently, isolating her: their love is "special, private — people won't understand, they'll be jealous or judgmental." So Linda doesn't tell her grown kids much, or her friends. The one relationship that's consuming her is the one no outsider gets to examine. He's also always *just* unable to meet — a canceled visit, a video call that "won't connect," a work crisis abroad. There's always a reason, and Linda, in love, supplies him the benefit of the doubt.

## STAGE 3 — The first crisis & the first ask

[B-ROLL: an urgent message — a medical emergency / a customs problem / a frozen account abroad; a request for help.]

**VO:** Then comes the first crisis. An emergency abroad — a medical bill, a customs hold on his equipment, a frozen account — and he's desperate, embarrassed to ask, but could she help, just temporarily? He'll pay her back the moment he's home. It's not a huge sum. She's in love; he's in trouble; of course she helps. The first ask is always modest — a foot in the door.

## STAGE 4 — Escalation & the hot-and-cold

[B-ROLL: more crises, larger sums; David alternating intense affection with sudden coldness when she hesitates.]

**VO:** After the first payment, the crises keep coming, and the sums grow. And now a darker dynamic appears: when Linda hesitates or asks questions, David grows cold, hurt, distant — *how could she not trust him, after everything?* Then, when she relents, the warmth floods back, more intense than ever. This unpredictable cycle of affection and withdrawal — **intermittent reinforcement** — bonds her even tighter, and trains her that compliance brings back the love. Often the scam fuses with "investment": David lets her in on a *crypto opportunity*, helping her set up an account where her balance magically grows — the romance scam and the investment scam, merged (the "pig-butchering" hybrid).

## STAGE 5 — The wall & the aftermath

[B-ROLL: Linda's savings gone; David vanished or demanding a final "fee"; the crushing double loss.]

**VO:** Eventually Linda runs out of money, or tries to withdraw the "investment" and hits the wall of fees. And then David — or whoever was typing — is gone. Linda is left with a double devastation that's hard to overstate: she has lost her savings *and* the person she loved, who turns out never to have existed. The shame is so crushing that many victims never report it, never tell their families, and sometimes — heartbreakingly — refuse to believe it was fake, because the grief of that is too much. The scam wounds twice.

# PART TWO — THE BREAKDOWN

[GFX: section header — "HOW IT WAS DONE."]

**VO:** A romance scam is essentially the *intimate-manipulation* module (Module 8) run by a stranger for money. Let's name the techniques.

## 1. Target selection & love bombing

**VO:** Scammers target the lonely, the grieving, the isolated — often (though not only) older or recently bereaved people — because the hunger for connection is the lever (target selection, Episode 31). Then **love bombing** (Episode 30): the flood of attention and instant soulmate-level intimacy that creates fast, deep attachment and switches off skepticism. The "we're so alike" is **liking** via manufactured similarity (Episode 42) — they mirror whatever you reveal.

## 2. Isolation

**VO:** "Keep us private, people won't understand" is textbook **isolation** (Episodes 25, 56) — the universal first move of control. It does two things: removes the outside anchors who'd say "this is a scam," and makes the relationship feel uniquely special. Every scam in this whole series needs to separate you from people who'd reality-check you. If a new love interest discourages you from telling your friends and family, that alone is a screaming red flag.

## 3. The unmeetable partner & manufactured reasons

**VO:** The endless reasons they can't meet or video-call clearly are obvious in hindsight, but **emotional engineering** (Episode 8) supplies endless benefit of the doubt — when you're in love, your brain *wants* the story to be true and generates excuses for free. And increasingly, technology closes even this gap: **deepfakes and voice-cloning** (Episode 36) now let scammers fake video calls and phone calls, so "I heard his voice, I saw his face" is no longer proof. This is exactly why Episode 36 urged a family **code word**.

## 4. Foot-in-the-door, sunk cost & escalation

**VO:** The modest first request is **foot-in-the-door** (Episode 30); once you've sent money once, you're committed. Each subsequent payment is justified by the last — **commitment, consistency, and sunk cost** (Episode 41): "I've already helped him so much; I can't abandon him now; if I stop, I lose everything I've put in." The merged fake-investment "gains" add **fabricated rewards** and greed to love, doubling the hooks (see Case File #3).

## 5. Intermittent reinforcement & the trauma bond

**VO:** The hot-and-cold cycle is **intermittent reinforcement** (Episode 59) — and it's the engine that makes victims defend their scammer even against the evidence. The unpredictable withdrawal-and-return of affection creates an intense bond and trains compliance: hesitate and lose the love; comply and the warmth returns. By the end, questioning David feels like betraying the most important relationship in her life. That's not stupidity. That's a documented psychological trap.

## 6. Why intelligence doesn't protect you

**VO:** The crucial, compassionate point: romance scams work on smart, capable people *because they don't target intelligence.* They target loneliness and love — universal human vulnerabilities. A brilliant person in love is still in love, with all the wishful thinking and benefit-of-the-doubt that love brings. So the contempt people feel — "how could she be so stupid?" — is both wrong and dangerous, because that shame is exactly what keeps victims silent, isolated, and unable to seek help. The kinder and more accurate frame: she wasn't foolish; she was *hunted*, by professionals, through her best capacity — her ability to love.

## THE TELLS — recognizing a romance scam

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Romance Scams]
- **Too fast, too perfect.** Instant soulmate, intense early declarations of love (love bombing), uncanny similarity.
- **Can never meet or video-call clearly.** Endless plausible reasons; and remember faces/voices can now be faked.
- **"Keep us private."** Discouraging you from telling friends and family = isolation = red flag.
- **Eventually, money.** Any request to send money, gift cards, or crypto — especially with urgency or a crisis.
- **Hot and cold.** Affection that withdraws when you hesitate and floods back when you comply.
- **An "investment" they guide you into.** Romance + a too-good trading opportunity = pig-butchering.

**VO:** The defenses are simple to state and hard to apply in love — which is why you decide them *now*, in daylight, not in the moment: **Never send money to someone you haven't met in person.** Full stop. **Verify independently** (a reverse-image search of their photos often reveals a stolen identity). And above all, **break the isolation**: tell a trusted friend or family member about any online relationship *early*, and listen if they're worried. The scam needs you alone. Connection is the cure.

## TRY IT YOURSELF

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, two acts of love. First, talk with any single, lonely, or recently-bereaved person in your life about romance scams — not as a warning that they're foolish, but as "this happens to wonderful, smart people, and I want you to have someone to call." Second, make the agreement explicit: *if anyone online ever asks you for money, talk to me first — no judgment, ever.* That open door, free of shame, is the single thing most likely to stop a scam before the first dollar leaves.

## RECAP

**VO:** The romance scam runs Module 8's intimate-manipulation playbook for profit: target the lonely, love-bomb, isolate, supply endless reasons not to meet (now aided by deepfakes), open with a small ask, escalate via sunk cost, and bond the victim through intermittent reinforcement. Intelligence is no shield, because it targets love, not logic. Never send money to someone you haven't met; verify independently; and keep — and offer — a shame-free outside anchor.
