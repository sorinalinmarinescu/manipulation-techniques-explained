# Episode 41 — Commitment & Consistency
### The small "yes" that traps you into the big one

**Module 5 · Episode 41** · **Target runtime:** 12–14 min
**Anchor callback:** EP30's foot-in-the-door; EP38's phone-shop "you said you wanted a good camera."
**Logline:** We have a deep need to act consistently with what we've already said and done. Manipulators exploit it: extract a small, easy commitment, then leverage your desire to stay consistent to pull you into much larger ones — including throwing good money after bad (the sunk-cost trap).

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand the consistency drive and why public/written commitments bind hardest.
- Connect foot-in-the-door, lowball, bait-and-switch, and sunk cost as one family.
- Learn to escape gracefully when consistency is being used against you.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Commitment & consistency:** our drive to act in line with what we've already committed to — especially publicly or in writing — even when it no longer serves us.

**VO:** Cialdini found that once people commit to something — a stance, a choice, a small action — they feel internal and social pressure to behave consistently with it afterward. Consistency is usually a virtue: it makes us reliable and saves us from re-deciding everything constantly. But it's also a lever. Get someone to take a small step, and you can use their need to "stay consistent" to walk them much further than they'd ever have gone in one leap.

**VO:** We met the entry move back in the scams episode: **foot-in-the-door** — a tiny first yes that makes the next, bigger yes feel natural. This episode is the whole family.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a person who's planted a small flag labeled "I'm someone who cares about X," now defending ever-larger actions to match it.]

**VO:** Two forces drive it. **Self-image:** once you've said "I'm the kind of person who supports this," contradicting it feels like a crack in your identity, which is deeply uncomfortable. **Social proof of the self:** we want others to see us as consistent, not flaky, so a *public* commitment binds harder than a private one, and a *written* one harder still.

**VO:** That's why manipulators work to get commitments that are **active, public, and effortful** — because those grip the tightest. Getting you to *say it out loud*, *write it down*, or *tell others* isn't incidental; it's the trap closing. After that, every consistent next step feels like your own free choice, when really you're just defending a flag someone maneuvered you into planting.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — The family of tricks

[B-ROLL: montage — a salesperson agreeing a price then adding fees; a "free" trial requiring a card; a gym membership you keep "to not waste it."]

**VO:** Several classic tactics are all consistency in disguise.

**VO:** **Foot-in-the-door.** Small request first, big request after. Agree to the little thing, and you're primed to agree to the large one to stay consistent.

**VO:** **Lowball.** Get you to commit to a great deal, then change the terms for the worse *after* you're committed. Maddeningly, people often stick with it — because they've already decided, told their family, started imagining it. The commitment outlives the deal that created it.

**VO:** **Bait-and-switch.** Advertise an amazing offer to get you in the door, then "that's sold out, but look at this pricier one." You came committed to buying, so you buy.

**VO:** **The sunk-cost trap.** Once you've sunk money, time, or effort into something, you feel pressure to continue *to be consistent with that investment* — "I can't quit now, I've put so much in." But the past spending is gone whether you continue or not. Throwing good money after bad is consistency turned self-destructive. Manipulators (and our own minds) use it to keep us in bad deals, bad subscriptions, even bad relationships and losing bets.

## SEGMENT 4 — Escaping your own consistency

[GFX: a person calmly pulling up their own planted flag.]

**VO:** The defense is to give yourself explicit permission to be "inconsistent" when consistency is being used against you — because changing your mind with new information isn't flakiness, it's intelligence.

[ON SCREEN — THE CONSISTENCY DEFENSE:]
- **Watch the first small yes.** Know that tiny commitments are designed to grow. It's fine to decline even the small ask.
- **Re-decide from today.** For any ongoing commitment, ask: "Knowing what I know *now*, with the past already spent, would I choose this *today*?" If no, the only thing keeping you in is sunk cost.
- **Beware the changed terms.** If a deal worsens *after* you commit (lowball) or the advertised thing vanishes (bait-and-switch), treat your earlier commitment as void. It was made about a different offer.
- **Separate identity from the trap.** "I support this cause" doesn't obligate you to every escalating ask made in its name.

**VO:** Cialdini's gut-level tip is useful here: if your stomach tells you you're being walked, step-by-step, into something you don't want — trust it, and stop, even if stopping feels "inconsistent." The discomfort of changing course is small next to the cost of consistently marching off a cliff.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Commitment & Consistency]
- **A suspiciously small first ask.** Foot-in-the-door: the little yes is a setup for the big one.
- **Terms that change after you commit.** Lowball / bait-and-switch — your commitment is being exploited.
- **"I've come too far to quit."** Sunk-cost talking. The past is gone; decide from now.
- **Pressure to be public.** Being pushed to declare, sign, or tell others binds you harder — notice it.

**VO:** The one-liner: *the past is paid no matter what you do next — so decide every commitment as if from today.* Consistency should serve you, not cage you.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, find one thing you keep "because you've already put so much in" — a subscription, a project, a habit. Ask the magic question: "Starting fresh today, with that cost already gone, would I choose this now?" If the honest answer is no, you've caught a sunk-cost trap. Quitting it isn't waste — continuing is.

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Commitment and consistency turn a small yes into a big one and keep us trapped by what we've already invested. Foot-in-the-door, lowball, bait-and-switch, and sunk cost are one family. The escape: re-decide from today, and allow yourself to "change your mind" when new facts demand it.
