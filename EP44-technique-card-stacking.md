# Episode 44 — Card Stacking
### Telling the truth — but only the convenient half

**Module 5 · Episode 44** · **Target runtime:** 12–13 min
**Anchor callback:** The classic 1937 propaganda devices (Block 1), completing the set.
**Logline:** Card stacking is lying with the truth. You don't state a single false fact; you simply present only the facts that help your case and bury the ones that don't — cherry-picking evidence, hiding the downsides, and choosing statistics that flatter.

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand selective presentation: deceiving with true-but-incomplete information.
- Recognize cherry-picking, misleading statistics, and the buried fine print.
- Ask "what's missing?" as a reflex.

---

## COLD OPEN

[B-ROLL: a supplement ad — "9 out of 10 users saw results!"; tiny print: "in a company study of 12 people."]

**VO:** "Nine out of ten users saw results." Sounds impressive — and it might be perfectly true. But what's the study? How many people? Compared to what? Funded by whom? What about the people it didn't work for, or the side effects? Every one of those answers might sink the claim. None of them is in the ad. That's not lying with false facts. That's lying by leaving things out.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Card stacking:** presenting only the evidence that supports your case while omitting or hiding everything that contradicts it — so a one-sided picture looks like the whole truth.

**VO:** Card stacking is one of the seven classic propaganda devices from that 1937 list we've drawn on throughout — and it completes our set, alongside transfer, testimonial, bandwagon, glittering generalities, and name-calling. The metaphor is a rigged card game: the deck is stacked so you only ever see the cards that favor the dealer.

**VO:** Here's what makes it so slippery and so common: it can be done with **entirely true statements.** Nobody lies. They just curate. And because everything said is technically accurate, card stacking survives fact-checking — you can verify each claim and still come away with a completely false overall impression. It's the favorite tool of anyone who wants to deceive while keeping their hands clean.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a hand of cards; only the good ones are shown, the rest face-down.]

**VO:** Card stacking works because we judge based on the information *in front of us*, and we rarely think to ask about the information that *isn't*. Psychologists call this a kind of "what you see is all there is" — our minds build a story from the available facts and don't naturally account for the missing ones. If you're only shown the upside, your brain quietly assumes there isn't much downside.

**VO:** It also exploits our trust in specifics. A precise statistic — "nine out of ten," "clinically proven," "up to 50% off" — feels solid and scientific. But "up to 50% off" can mean one item is half-price and everything else is barely discounted. "Clinically proven" can rest on a tiny, self-funded study. The number is true; the impression is engineered. The con is in the framing and the omissions, not the digits.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the wild

[B-ROLL: montage — cherry-picked graphs; "studies show"; a résumé with gaps; a political ad citing only flattering numbers.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** Selective testimonials (you see the raves, not the complaints), "up to" claims, statistics with no context, and the fine print that quietly contradicts the big print. The famous example: "4 out of 5 dentists recommend" — recommend *what*, surveyed *how*, out of *how many*?

**VO:** **Politics.** This is the daily bread of spin. Cite the economic numbers that flatter your side and ignore the ones that don't. Quote your opponent's worst out-of-context line and skip their actual position (a cousin of the straw man we'll meet in Module 6). Pick the start date for a chart that makes your record look best — a stacked deck of *time*.

**VO:** **Statistics.** The most powerful card stacking hides in numbers. Choosing the flattering measure (total vs. per-person, gross vs. net), the flattering baseline, the flattering time window. Each number can be true, and the chart still lies. We touched this under framing (Episode 9); here it's the deliberate art of showing only the helpful figures.

## SEGMENT 4 — The "what's missing" reflex

[GFX: a person turning over the face-down cards.]

**VO:** The defense is a single, trainable reflex: whenever a case seems airtight and one-sided, ask **"what's missing?"** A stacked deck only works while the other cards stay face-down.

[ON SCREEN — THE CARD-STACKING DEFENSE:]
- **Look for the other side.** Honest persuasion acknowledges trade-offs and counter-evidence. A presentation with *zero* downsides is a warning sign, not a strong case.
- **Interrogate the statistic.** Compared to what? Out of how many? Measured how? Funded by whom? Over what time period?
- **Read the fine print and the footnotes.** "Up to," "results not typical," "in a company-sponsored study" — the truth is often hiding in small type.
- **Seek a source with no stake.** Find evaluations from people who *don't* benefit if you say yes.

**VO:** The mental shift is to stop asking only "is what they said true?" and start asking "is what they said the *whole* relevant truth?" Those are very different questions, and card stacking lives in the gap between them.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Card Stacking]
- **Suspiciously one-sided.** All upside, no trade-offs? Reality has trade-offs. Someone's hiding cards.
- **Context-free statistics.** "9 out of 10," "up to 50%," "clinically proven" with no details.
- **Cherry-picked comparisons.** Flattering start dates, hand-picked examples, the best-case treated as typical.
- **Fine print fights the headline.** When the small text contradicts the big text, believe the small text.

**VO:** The one-liner: *they don't have to lie if they can just leave things out — so always ask what's missing.*

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, take one impressive statistic from an ad or a headline and play detective. Find the source, the sample size, the comparison, and who paid for it. Then ask what *isn't* shown. Sometimes the claim holds up beautifully. Often, the missing cards change everything. Either way, you're building the most important reflex against polished, "factual" manipulation.

---

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Card stacking deceives with true-but-incomplete information: cherry-picked facts, context-free statistics, buried downsides. It beats fact-checking because nothing said is false. The defense is the "what's missing?" reflex — demand the other side, the context, and the fine print.
