# Episode 45 — Plain Folks
### When the powerful pretend to be just like you

**Module 5 · Episode 45** · **Target runtime:** 11–12 min
**Anchor callback:** The 1937 propaganda devices; the flip side of borrowed authority (EP3).
**Logline:** "Plain folks" is the technique of the powerful or wealthy presenting themselves as ordinary, down-to-earth people just like their audience — to borrow the trust we extend to "people like us."

**Learning objectives:**
- Recognize the manufactured "ordinariness" of elites and brands.
- Understand why "people like us" lowers our guard.
- Separate relatability from the actual substance of what's offered.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Plain folks:** a technique in which a powerful, wealthy, or elite person or organization presents itself as ordinary and relatable, to win the trust people give to "their own kind."

**VO:** "Plain folks" is another of the classic 1937 propaganda devices, and it's the mirror image of borrowed authority from Episode 3. Authority says, "trust me, I'm an expert above you." Plain folks says, "trust me, I'm just like you." Both are bids for trust — opposite costumes, same goal.

**VO:** The technique trades on a real and reasonable instinct: we trust people who share our background, values, and struggles, because they're more likely to understand and represent us. The manipulation is when that ordinariness is **performed** by someone whose real life is nothing like yours — the politician who's never worried about a bill posing as a working stiff, the global brand cosplaying as your neighbor.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a callback to in-group/out-group (EP16) — "one of us" lights up the trust center.]

**VO:** This lever plugs straight into the in-group instinct we covered in the us-vs-them episode. We're wired to trust and favor members of our own group. "They're one of us" flips a switch: this person is on my side, shares my interests, won't exploit me. So a powerful figure who can convincingly seem ordinary borrows all the goodwill we reserve for our own.

**VO:** It also disarms a specific suspicion. We're naturally a little wary of the rich and powerful — we suspect their interests differ from ours. Plain folks defuses exactly that wariness. By eating the hot dog, dropping the "g"s, wearing the work boots, the elite figure says, "don't worry, I'm not one of *those* powerful people — I'm one of you." It's a preemptive strike against the entirely sensible question: *does this person actually share my interests?*

## SEGMENT 3 — In the wild

[B-ROLL: montage — campaign diners and pickup trucks; "down to earth" celebrity branding; "small batch / family owned" packaging from conglomerates.]

**VO:** **Politics.** The diner photo-op, the rolled-up sleeves, the dropped formality, the carefully folksy turns of phrase, the "I'm just a regular person who worries about the same things you do." Sometimes it's sincere; often it's a costume tested by consultants. Watch for the gap between the *image* of ordinariness and the *substance* of whose interests the person actually serves.

**VO:** **Branding.** "Family owned." "Small batch." "Humble beginnings." "We're just folks who love good coffee." Enormous corporations spend fortunes manufacturing a homespun, scrappy, local image precisely because we trust the little guy more than the giant. The rustic packaging and the founder's "story" are marketing, not autobiography.

**VO:** **Influencers and the wealthy.** The "relatable" celebrity who shows you their "normal" day (filmed in a mansion), the rich entrepreneur who emphasizes how they "started with nothing." Relatability is a brand asset, and it's carefully produced.

## SEGMENT 4 — Relatability vs. representation

[GFX: a split — "seems like me (image)" vs. "acts in my interest (substance)."]

**VO:** The defense is to separate two things that plain folks deliberately blurs: *seeming* like you, and *acting in your interest.* They are not the same, and one is theater while the other is what actually matters.

[ON SCREEN — THE PLAIN-FOLKS DEFENSE:]
- **Judge actions, not costumes.** Forget the flannel and the hot dog. What has this person or company actually *done*, and whose interests do their decisions serve?
- **Mind the gap.** The bigger the distance between someone's real life/power and their "ordinary" image, the more the image is a performance.
- **"Relatable" isn't "right."** Liking that someone seems like you tells you nothing about whether their policy, product, or pitch is good for you.
- **Ask who benefits.** Cui bono, again. The hot dog is free for them; the vote or the purchase is the real transaction.

**VO:** You can enjoy that a leader seems down-to-earth or that a brand has a charming story — just don't let the *feeling* of kinship substitute for the *evidence* of whether they're actually on your side. Plenty of genuinely ordinary-seeming people serve interests very far from yours.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Plain Folks]
- **Manufactured ordinariness.** A powerful/wealthy figure conspicuously performing "regular person."
- **Image over record.** Lots of relatability signaling, little about actual actions or interests.
- **"Family owned / small batch" from a giant.** Homespun branding masking a large corporation.
- **The gap test.** How far is the real life from the projected image? The wider the gap, the bigger the act.

**VO:** The one-liner: *seeming like you is not the same as being for you* — judge whose side someone's *actions* are on.
