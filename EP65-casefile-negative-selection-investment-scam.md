# Episode 65 — CASE FILE #3: The Negative-Selection Scam
### The dumb, obvious ad that's smarter than it looks — and the dark-pattern trap behind it

**The Case Files** · **Type:** Two-part worked example · **Target runtime:** up to 30 min
**Logline:** Exactly the campaign you might have wondered about: a deliberately *stupid*, obviously-fake "get rich" ad — built to repel anyone smart enough to doubt it — funneling the people it *does* catch into a slick, dark-pattern website engineered to extract escalating "investments." **Part 1** follows a composite victim, Robert, from a cringeworthy ad to a drained bank account. **Part 2** breaks down the two-stage machine: filter, then fleece.

> **Composite dramatization** built from documented patterns in online investment fraud, "get-rich-quick" funnels, and crypto/"pig-butchering" scams. "WealthPulse AI," Robert, and "Marcus" are inventions. Real losses are enormous: U.S. consumers reported **$1.3 billion** in romance-scam losses in 2022, and the FBI reported **$2.57 billion** in crypto-investment-scam losses that year — older adults hardest hit.

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand a *two-stage* scam: negative selection (filter) + dark-pattern funnel (fleece).
- See why "obviously fake" is a feature, and why mocking victims misses the point.
- Recognize the funnel and protect the people it targets.

Today we follow one of those hands — and dissect a scam in two ruthless stages: first it *filters*, then it *fleeces*.

# PART ONE — THE CAMPAIGN UNFOLDS

## STAGE 1 — The filter (the "dumb" ad)

[B-ROLL: Robert, 62, recently retired, worried about his savings, pauses on the garish ad instead of scrolling.]

**VO:** Meet Robert. He's 62, recently retired, anxious that his savings won't last, and not very experienced online. Where you saw a laughable scam, Robert sees hope — a regular guy who "cracked the code." The very crudeness that repelled you *reassures* him: it feels homemade, unpolished, real, not like those slick corporate ads he distrusts. He clicks.

**VO:** Notice what just happened at the population level. Out of a million people who saw that ad, the skeptical, the cautious, the financially savvy — they all scrolled away, exactly as designed. The tiny fraction who clicked have *pre-selected themselves* as hopeful, trusting, perhaps desperate, perhaps isolated. The scammer hasn't wasted a second on the rest. The filter did its job.

## STAGE 2 — The landing page (the dark-pattern trap)

[B-ROLL: a slick "WealthPulse AI" site — countdown timer, "live" profit ticker, fake news logos, pop-ups, a single glowing "CLAIM YOUR SPOT" button.]

**VO:** The crude ad leads somewhere surprisingly *slick*: "WealthPulse AI," a polished site promising an automated trading system that "can't lose." And now the environment turns into a pressure machine. A countdown timer screams the offer expires in 9 minutes. A ticker shows other people "earning" in real time. Logos claim it's been "featured" on real news networks. A pop-up warns "only 3 spots left in your area." A chat window lights up: a friendly "advisor," Marcus, is here to *personally* help Robert get started. Every element is engineered to rush him past thought and toward one glowing button.

## STAGE 3 — The small first step

[B-ROLL: Robert making a small initial "deposit"; a dashboard showing his balance magically growing.]

**VO:** Marcus is warm, patient, encouraging — not pushy. He suggests Robert start small, just to "see it work." A modest deposit — a few hundred dollars. And then the dashboard does something intoxicating: Robert's balance starts *climbing*. He's "up" 40% by the weekend. He's elated. He tells no one, because Marcus has gently warned that "jealous people and the mainstream banks" won't understand. Of course, the dashboard is a fiction — numbers on a screen the scammers fully control. But to Robert, it's proof. It's working.

## STAGE 4 — Escalation

[B-ROLL: Marcus encouraging larger deposits; Robert moving retirement savings; the "balance" now showing huge gains.]

**VO:** Now the real extraction begins. Buoyed by his "gains," and encouraged by Marcus ("imagine if you'd started bigger"), Robert deposits more. Then more. He moves chunks of his retirement savings. The fake balance soars into the hundreds of thousands — on screen. Robert feels brilliant, lucky, finally secure. Each deposit makes the next feel safer; after all, look how well it's going. He's all in, emotionally and financially.

## STAGE 5 — The wall, and the second sting

[B-ROLL: Robert tries to withdraw; "tax/fee required to release funds"; then silence.]

**VO:** Then Robert tries to withdraw his "winnings." Suddenly there's a problem: a "tax," a "release fee," a "verification deposit" required before he can access the money — a final demand that he pay *more* to get out what he thinks he has. Desperate not to lose his huge (imaginary) balance, many victims pay even this. And then — Marcus stops replying. The site vanishes or freezes. The money — the real money Robert deposited — is simply gone, and was gone the moment he sent it. There were never any gains. There was only the filter, and the funnel, and Robert.

# PART TWO — THE BREAKDOWN

[GFX: section header — "HOW IT WAS DONE." Two big labels: "STAGE A: FILTER" and "STAGE B: FLEECE."]

**VO:** This scam has two distinct machines bolted together. Let's take them in turn.

## STAGE A — The filter: negative selection

**VO:** The "dumb" ad is **negative selection** (Episode 32) in its purest form. The scammer's scarce resource isn't ad views — those are cheap — it's the *time* spent working a victim. The nightmare is a skeptic who engages, wastes the scammer's effort, and never pays. So the ad is *deliberately* crude and unbelievable, to repel everyone except the most susceptible. The absurdity isn't incompetence; it's a sorting machine that makes the best victims **self-select** and screens everyone else out for free.

[LOWER THIRD: "By repelling all but the most gullible, the scammer gets the best marks to self-select" — paraphrasing Herley (2012)]

**VO:** This is why mocking the ad — "who'd fall for THAT?" — completely misses the point, and is exactly the reaction the scammer wants from you. You were *supposed* to scroll past. And it's why contempt for victims is both cruel and wrong: Robert wasn't stupid, he was *selected* — sorted out by a filter designed to find someone hopeful, trusting, and unguarded. The crude ad also borrowed a little **plain folks** (Episode 45) — the "regular guy, not slick corporate" vibe — to feel trustworthy to its target.

## STAGE B — The fleece: the dark-pattern funnel

**VO:** Once you're through the filter, the slick site unloads nearly every technique in this series at once. Watch them stack.

**VO:** **Manufactured scarcity & urgency** (Episode 40): the countdown timer, "3 spots left." **Fake social proof** (Episode 4): the live "earnings" ticker, testimonials, "others in your area." **Borrowed authority** (Episode 3): the fake "as featured on" news logos. **Dark patterns** (Episode 54): the whole page is engineered — the single glowing button, the pop-ups, the friction-free path *in*. **Liking & the third-party "advisor"** (Episodes 42, 6): Marcus is warm and "on your side," not a salesman. **Isolation** (Episodes 25, 56): "don't tell jealous people or the banks" — cutting Robert off from anyone who'd reality-check him, the universal move.

**VO:** Then the extraction uses the deepest levers. The small first deposit is **foot-in-the-door** (Episode 30). The rising fake balance is a **fabricated reward** that exploits greed and hope and manufactures trust. The escalation is **commitment, consistency, and sunk cost** (Episode 41) — each deposit justifies the next, and "I've put in so much, I can't stop now" keeps Robert feeding the machine. The "pay a fee to release your funds" finale is a brutal second sting that weaponizes that same sunk cost against him one last time.

## Why the two stages fit together

[GFX: a funnel — crude ad at the wide top filters to a trickle; the dark-pattern site squeezes that trickle dry.]

**VO:** Here's the elegant, awful logic. Stage A (the filter) is *cheap and wide* — blast a stupid ad at millions, spend nothing on the skeptics. Stage B (the fleece) is *expensive and narrow* — slick site, a human "advisor" giving personal attention — so it's only spent on the pre-qualified few who self-selected as likely to pay. The scammer optimizes the whole pipeline: repel the many for free, then lavish effort on the vulnerable few. It's a factory, and Robert was its product.

**VO:** And the scale is staggering, which is why this matters. These investment-and-romance scams (often called "pig-butchering," because victims are "fattened up" with fake gains before slaughter) cost people billions every year — the FBI reported over two and a half billion dollars in crypto-investment-scam losses in a single year, with older adults hit hardest. Robert is one of hundreds of thousands.

## THE TELLS — spotting the two-stage scam

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: The Filter-and-Fleece Scam]
- **"Who'd fall for that?"** That reaction means you may be looking at a *filter*. Don't relax — warn the people it's hunting.
- **Guaranteed/huge returns, "can't lose."** Real investments carry risk; guaranteed riches are the oldest lie there is.
- **Urgency + fake crowd + fake authority.** Countdown timers, live "profit" tickers, "as seen on" logos — manufactured.
- **A friendly "advisor" + "don't tell anyone."** Personal attention plus isolation from anyone who'd check it.
- **Small deposit, magic gains, then "deposit more."** The fattening stage. The balance is a number they control.
- **A fee to withdraw your "winnings."** A dead giveaway of a scam — and the second sting.

**VO:** The two unbeatable defenses: First, **if you can't withdraw it freely, it isn't yours** — a balance you can't cash out is just a number on someone else's screen. Second, **break the isolation**: any "investment" that tells you not to talk to your family, your real bank, or a licensed advisor is, by that very instruction, telling you it's a scam. Talk to someone outside before you send a cent.

## TRY IT YOURSELF

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, do the most useful thing in this whole episode: talk to an older or less-online relative about *exactly* this two-stage scam — the dumb ad, the slick site, the friendly advisor, the rising fake balance, the withdrawal "fee." Tell them the one rule that defeats it: *before sending money to any online investment, call me first.* You can't unbuild the scammer's funnel. But you can be the outside anchor that stops the person they're hunting. That conversation can save someone their life savings.

## RECAP

**VO:** The negative-selection scam is two machines: a *filter* — a deliberately dumb ad that repels skeptics so the vulnerable self-select — bolted to a *fleece* — a dark-pattern site stacking scarcity, fake proof, isolation, foot-in-the-door, fabricated gains, and sunk cost to extract escalating "investments," capped by a withdrawal-fee second sting. The "obvious fakeness" is the design. Don't mock the victims; warn them — and remember: if you can't withdraw it, it isn't yours.
