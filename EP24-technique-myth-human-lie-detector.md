# Episode 24 — The Myth of the Human Lie Detector
### Why almost no one can spot a liar — and why that's where it all goes wrong

**Module 3 · Episode 24 of the Reid arc** · **Target runtime:** 14–15 min
**Anchor callback:** EP23 — the whole technique turns on a detective first *deciding* you're lying.
**Logline:** The entire Reid machine starts with one judgment: "this person is lying." This episode shows that humans — including trained professionals — are barely better than a coin toss at detecting lies, and that the popular "tells" (shifty eyes, looking up-left, fidgeting, the polygraph) are myths. If the starting judgment is junk, everything built on it is junk.

**Learning objectives:**
- Know the real, cited accuracy of human lie detection (spoiler: ~coin toss).
- Debunk the big "tells": eye direction, gaze aversion, fidgeting, the polygraph, voice-stress, "statement analysis."
- Understand why *confidence* in spotting lies is more dangerous than the inaccuracy itself.

**VO:** We all think we can tell. He won't make eye contact — guilty. She looked up and to the left before answering — making it up. He's fidgeting, he's sweating, his story's too smooth. We are *certain* we can read a liar.

**VO:** Here's the uncomfortable truth, measured across hundreds of studies and thousands of people: we are almost exactly as good at detecting lies as we'd be by flipping a coin. And the experts? Barely better — but far more confident. That gap, between how good we *think* we are and how good we *are*, is where innocent people start down the road to a false confession.

## SEGMENT 1 — The number that should change your mind

[ON SCREEN — big stat: "54%"]

**VO:** In 2006, two researchers, Charles Bond and Bella DePaulo, combined the results of more than 200 experiments on detecting deception — tens of thousands of judgments. The headline finding: when people try to tell truths from lies with no special aids, they're right about **54 percent** of the time. A coin toss is 50. We are four points better than chance.

[LOWER THIRD: Bond & DePaulo (2006), meta-analysis of 200+ studies · avg. accuracy ≈ 54%]

**VO:** It gets more specific, and more revealing. On average people correctly spot only about 47 percent of *lies* — worse than chance — and about 61 percent of *truths*. Why the lopsidedness? Because most people, most of the time, lean toward believing others. We're built with a "truth bias," which is actually a fine way to live — but it means we miss a lot of lies.

**VO:** "But surely trained professionals are better?" That's the part that should worry you. Studies find that police officers, detectives, judges, and similar "professional lie-catchers" score around 55 percent — statistically about the same as students and ordinary citizens. Training doesn't reliably help. What training *does* do is make people more *confident*. And confident-but-wrong is the most dangerous combination in this entire field.

[LOWER THIRD: Professionals ≈ no more accurate than laypeople, but more confident · meta-analytic findings]

## SEGMENT 2 — Why we're so bad at it

[GFX: two columns — "What we THINK liars do" vs. "What research finds."]

**VO:** The reason is simple and humbling: there is no reliable physical "tell" for lying. No universal signal your body sends when you deceive. The cues we all "know" — looking away, fidgeting, hesitating, touching your face — show up in nervous *honest* people just as much as in liars. An innocent person being accused of a crime is terrified. Terror looks a lot like what we imagine guilt looks like. So the anxious truth-teller gets read as the liar.

**VO:** Worse, liars often know the stereotypes and do the opposite — they hold eye contact, sit still, keep their story smooth. So the "obvious tells" can be backwards: the calm, steady person is lying and the sweaty, fidgety one is telling the truth. When the signal is noise, reading it harder doesn't help. It just makes you confidently wrong.

## SEGMENT 3 — The specific myths (including the one you asked about)

[GFX: a "MYTH BUSTED" stamp that hits each item.]

**VO:** Let's kill the big ones by name, because manipulators and amateur "experts" still trade in them.

**VO:** **Myth one — "look up and to the left."** There's a popular belief, borrowed from something called neuro-linguistic programming, that eye direction reveals lying — look one way and you're remembering something real, the other way and you're inventing it. It's everywhere, from TV to corporate seminars. In 2012, researchers led by Richard Wiseman tested it directly: they coded the eye movements of people telling truths and lies, and found **no relationship** whatsoever. They even tried teaching people the eye-direction rule to see if it improved their lie detection. It didn't. The "lying eyes" theory is, in the researchers' view, something the public and professionals should simply abandon.

[LOWER THIRD: "Lying eyes"/NLP eye-direction myth debunked — Wiseman et al., PLOS ONE (2012)]

**VO:** And here's the deliciously absurd part you flagged: because the myth is so widespread, some people deliberately glance "up and to the left" to *seem* truthful — gaming a signal that was never real in the first place. So you have liars faking a tell that doesn't work, watched by accusers who believe in a tell that doesn't exist. Everyone's performing a superstition.

**VO:** **Myth two — gaze aversion, fidgeting, nervousness.** As we said: these are signs of *stress*, not *deception*. Useless for telling them apart.

**VO:** **Myth three — the polygraph, the "lie detector."** This machine measures your heart rate, breathing, and sweat — your body's arousal. But arousal isn't lying; it's just arousal. A guilty person can stay calm; an innocent, frightened person can spike. In 2003, the U.S. National Academy of Sciences reviewed the evidence and concluded that the physiological responses the polygraph measures are **not uniquely related to deception**, and that its accuracy is too low to justify relying on it for security screening. That's why polygraph results are inadmissible in many courts. The "lie detector" largely detects fear.

[LOWER THIRD: Polygraph not a valid lie detector — U.S. National Academy of Sciences (2003), "The Polygraph and Lie Detection"]

**VO:** **Myth four — "voice-stress analyzers" and "statement analysis."** Gadgets that claim to hear lies in your voice, and systems that claim to find deception in your exact word choice. Neither has solid scientific support as a lie detector. They share the polygraph's fatal flaw: they detect *something* — stress, word patterns — and then assume that something means lying.

## SEGMENT 4 — Why this is the rotten foundation of the whole technique

[GFX: a building labeled "INTERROGATION" sitting on a cracked foundation labeled "we can tell you're lying."]

**VO:** Now connect it back to episode 23. The Reid process has a stage — the "behavior analysis interview" — where an investigator watches your behavior and decides whether you're being truthful. Only *after* deciding you're a liar do they switch into the accusatory interrogation designed to extract a confession.

**VO:** But we've just seen that this decision is made with tools that don't work — body-language reading that's barely better than a coin toss, performed by professionals whose main edge is overconfidence. So an innocent, nervous person can be misjudged as deceptive in stage two, and then subjected, in stage three, to hours of pressure built on the false premise that their guilt is already proven. The error at the start contaminates everything after it. Garbage in; confession out.

**VO:** That's the deep problem, and it's bigger than any one room. Any time someone says "I can tell you're lying" — a boss, a partner, a salesman, a TV "body-language expert" — remember the number. Fifty-four percent. They mostly can't. They just think they can.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Junk Lie-Detection]
- **"I can tell you're lying."** Treat this as a *power move*, not a fact. Almost no one can, and confidence is not accuracy.
- **Beware the "tell."** Eye direction, fidgeting, eye contact, sweating — stress signals, not lie signals.
- **The machine isn't magic.** Polygraphs and voice analyzers detect arousal, not honesty.
- **Watch for the flip.** A claim that you're being deceptive is often the *opening move* of a pressure campaign — it sets up everything that follows.

**VO:** The defense is to refuse the frame. When someone announces they've detected your dishonesty, you don't have to accept that as established fact and start defending yourself against it. It's an opinion formed with broken tools — and, very often, a deliberate tactic to put you on the back foot.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, notice every time someone claims to *know* what another person is really feeling or hiding — on a reality show, in a courtroom drama, in an argument, in a "body-language breakdown" video. Ask: what are they actually basing that on? You'll find it's almost always a "tell" we've just debunked. And next time *you* feel sure someone's lying from their body language — pause, and remember you're probably running at about coin-toss odds.

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Humans are near-chance lie detectors; professionals are barely better but far more confident; and the famous "tells" — eye direction, fidgeting, the polygraph — are myths. That broken judgment is the foundation the whole interrogation is built on. Crack the foundation, and you understand why the building is dangerous.
