# Episode 27 — Crushing Denials & the Alternative Question
### How they take away your "no" — then close the trap with a fake choice

**Module 3 · Episode 27 of the Reid arc** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** EP26 — the sympathetic "theme" is on the table; now the technique removes your ability to refuse it.
**Logline:** Steps three through seven of the Reid technique do two things: they systematically shut down your denials and objections until you fall silent, and then they spring the "alternative question" — a two-option trap where *both* answers are a confession. It's the false dilemma from Episode 19, weaponized to extract an admission.

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand how denials and objections are deliberately cut off.
- See the "alternative question" as a false dilemma engineered so every door leads to "guilty."
- Recognize the take-away-your-no and fake-choice moves in sales and everyday pressure.

## SEGMENT 1 — Taking away your "no"

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Handling denials / overcoming objections (steps 3–4):** systematically interrupting, dismissing, and talking past the suspect's denials and reasons, so resistance can't build momentum.

**VO:** Steps three and four are about your resistance — and crushing it. The manual's logic is blunt: a suspect who's allowed to repeat "I didn't do it" grows more confident and harder to move. So denials are to be cut off *early*, before they gain strength. "Let me finish." "We're past that." "I don't want to hear it again."

**VO:** Objections — your actual reasons, your logic, your alibi — are treated not as information to weigh but as obstacles to remove. The interrogator has an answer ready for each one, turning your reasons back on you or simply brushing them aside. Notice the deep move here: a real conversation invites your denials and reasons. This process is engineered to *prevent* them. You are being trained, in real time, that speaking up changes nothing.

**VO:** And it connects straight to power. Taking away a person's ability to say "no" — to be heard at all — is profoundly disorienting. We rely on the word "no" as our basic defense. Strip it away for hours and many people sink into the passive, hopeless state the technique is steering them toward: steps five and six, where the interrogator closes the physical distance, holds eye contact, and moves in as the suspect goes quiet, deflated, and ready to give up.

## SEGMENT 2 — The cruelest move: the alternative question

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**The alternative question (step 7):** offering the suspect two choices about the crime — one face-saving, one ugly — where *both* options assume guilt.

**VO:** Now comes the masterpiece of the whole technique, step seven, and it's a trap so elegant it deserves a slow look. After you've been worn down and gone quiet, the interrogator offers what sounds like a gentle, easy question — a simple choice between two options:

[GFX: two doors. Door A: "You planned it, cold and deliberate." Door B: "It just happened in the heat of the moment." Below both, in red: "...either way, you did it."]

**DETECTIVE (soft):** David, I don't think you're a cold person. So which was it — did you plan the whole thing out... or did it just happen, in the moment, and things got out of hand?

**VO:** Look at the two doors. One is ugly — you're a calculating villain. One is sympathetic — you're a decent person who slipped. Naturally, the worn-down suspect reaches for the kinder option. But here's the trap: **both options are confessions.** "It just happened in the moment" still means *you did it.* The question was never "did you do this?" That's been quietly erased. The only choice on offer is *which kind* of guilty you are.

**VO:** This is the false dilemma from episode 19, sharpened into a weapon. Remember the rule from that episode: when you're handed exactly two options, look for the hidden third. Here the hidden option — the one the whole interrogation has worked to make invisible — is "I didn't do it at all." By the time the alternative question arrives, that door has been walled off: your denials were crushed, your reasons dismissed, your hope drained. So you pick the gentle lie, thinking you're choosing the lesser evil. You've just confessed.

## SEGMENT 3 — Why an exhausted, innocent person takes the bait

[GFX: the rigged scale from EP26 returns; the "kind" option glows invitingly.]

**VO:** Put it all together and you can see how the impossible happens. An innocent person, hours in, isolated, told the evidence is overwhelming, denials no longer permitted, is offered a soft landing — "just say it happened the understandable way." It feels like the *least bad* move available. It feels like the way out. And crucially, the alternative question lets them confess without first overcoming their own shame, because the "theme" already made the act sound forgivable.

**VO:** They're not agreeing because they're guilty. They're agreeing because every other door has been shut, and this one appears to lead outside. The signature, the "yes," the chosen door — it's the product of the trap, not of the truth.

## SEGMENT 4 — The take-away-your-no and the fake choice, in the wild

[B-ROLL: montage — a salesperson "so would you prefer the red one or the blue one?"; a pushy pitch that never lets you say no; "so should we start Monday or Wednesday?"]

**VO:** Both moves live far outside the interrogation room.

**VO:** **The assumptive close.** Classic high-pressure sales never asks "do you want to buy?" — that invites a "no." Instead: "Would you prefer to put down the deposit today or tomorrow?" "Should we set you up with the standard or the premium?" Both answers assume you've already bought. It's the alternative question wearing a sales blazer: erase the real decision, offer two versions of "yes."

**VO:** **Never letting you say no.** Pushy persuaders talk over your objections, have a counter for every reason, and treat your "no" as a hurdle rather than an answer — exactly like steps three and four. If someone seems incapable of hearing "no," that's not enthusiasm; it's technique.

**VO:** **Manufactured either/or.** "You're either in or you're out." "It's now or never." The fake binary that hides every other option — buy later, walk away, choose something else entirely.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Denials & Fake Choices]
- **Can you actually say no?** If your objections are being talked over and your "no" won't land, the conversation is a pressure process, not an exchange.
- **Two options, both their way?** "A or B" where both mean yes is the alternative-question trap. Ask: what's the option they erased?
- **The kind door.** Be most suspicious of the *sympathetic* choice offered right after you've been worn down — it's bait, not mercy.
- **Restore the real question.** "Did I agree to buy/admit/commit at all?" is almost always the real question they've hidden.

**VO:** The defense is to keep your "no" alive and to always name the erased option out loud. "Neither." "I'm not choosing between those." "The real question isn't which one — it's whether at all, and my answer is no." A trap built on two doors collapses the instant you insist on the third: walking out the way you came in.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, hunt for the "assumptive close" and the two-option trap. When a salesperson, an app, or anyone asks you to choose between A and B, pause and ask whether you ever agreed to the thing both options assume. Practice the cleanest sentence in self-defense: "Neither — I haven't decided to do this at all." If saying it makes the other person frustrated or pushy, congratulations: you've just identified a trap, and escaped it.
