# Episode 37 — Manipulating the Information Environment
### Gaming search, autocomplete, and "data voids"

**Module 4 (finale) · Episode 37** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** EP31 — shaping the path you travel when *you* go looking.
**Logline:** Sometimes the manipulation isn't a message pushed at you — it's a trap laid where you'll go searching. This episode covers how manipulators shape search results, exploit "data voids," seed autocomplete and hashtags, and plant the phrases they want you to look up, so that your own research leads you exactly where they want.

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand that the act of "doing your own research" can be manipulated.
- Learn what data voids are and how they're exploited.
- Build habits for searching that don't walk into the trap.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Information-environment manipulation:** shaping what people find when they search or browse — through gaming search results, exploiting gaps in available information, and seeding the terms people look up.

**VO:** Every other technique in this module pushes content *toward* you. This one is sneakier: it lies in wait where you'll go *looking*. Because we trust things we "found ourselves" far more than things pushed at us, manipulating your search is incredibly powerful. You feel like an independent investigator; you're actually walking a path someone paved.

**VO:** Search engines and feeds rank and autocomplete based on signals that can be gamed. And there are gaps in the world's information that bad actors can rush to fill before anyone honest does.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Data voids: the trap in the gaps

[GFX: a search bar; an obscure phrase typed in; only fringe results appear.]

**VO:** Here's the key concept, from researchers Michael Golebiewski and danah boyd, who coined the term "data void" in 2018. A data void is a search term for which there's little good information — usually because it's new, obscure, or rarely searched. Most of the time that's harmless. But it becomes a weapon when manipulators *create or exploit* the void.

[LOWER THIRD: "Data void" — Golebiewski & boyd, Data & Society (2018)]

**VO:** The play works like this. A manipulator invents or popularizes a specific, unusual phrase — a coined slogan, a fake-scientific term, a loaded question — and fills the void with their own content. Then they push people to search that exact phrase: "look up [obscure term]." Because almost no one else has written about it, *their* material dominates the results. The searcher types it in, sees a wall of seemingly independent confirmation, and concludes it must be true — when in reality they were sent into an empty room that the manipulator had already decorated.

**VO:** It's brilliant precisely because it hijacks good instincts. "Look it up yourself" is supposed to be how you resist manipulation. Data-void exploitation turns that defense into a delivery system.

## SEGMENT 3 — The other tools: SEO, autocomplete, and seeded terms

[B-ROLL: montage — a flood of near-identical articles; an autocomplete suggestion completing a loaded phrase; a coordinated hashtag.]

**VO:** Data voids are one method; here are the others in the same family.

**VO:** **Gaming the rankings (manipulative SEO).** Search engines rank pages partly by signals like links and freshness. Coordinated actors can flood the zone with many pages pushing one narrative, juicing those signals so their version rises to the top — making a fringe claim look like the consensus answer.

**VO:** **Autocomplete and "suggested" steering.** The phrases a search box suggests as you type, or the "related searches" offered, shape where people go. If manipulators can make a loaded phrase trend, it can start appearing as a "suggestion," nudging ordinary users toward it. The suggestion feels neutral; it isn't always.

**VO:** **Seeding the search term itself.** This is the master move that ties it together — and we glimpsed it in the dog-whistle and bot episodes. Whoever decides *which words you go looking with* has already shaped what you'll find. "Search this exact phrase" is rarely innocent when the phrase is unusual and emotionally loaded. The choice of query is half the answer.

## SEGMENT 4 — How to search without being steered

[GFX: a "smart searching" checklist animates.]

**VO:** "Do your own research" is still good advice — but only if you research *well*. Here's how not to walk into the trap.

[ON SCREEN — SMART SEARCHING:]
- **Don't search the exact loaded phrase you were handed.** Rephrase it neutrally, in your own words. If someone insists you use *their* exact term, ask why.
- **Search the topic, not the slogan.** Look up the underlying subject broadly, not the manipulator's branded keyword.
- **Check who's behind the top results.** A wall of agreement from sites you've never heard of is a warning, not a confirmation.
- **Look for credible, independent sources** — established outlets, primary documents, subject experts — not just whatever ranks first.
- **Notice thin results.** If a dramatic claim only appears in fringe or low-quality pages, that "data void" emptiness is itself information: the claim may be manufactured.
- **Triangulate.** Real findings show up across multiple independent, reputable sources. One-source "truths" deserve doubt.

**VO:** The mindset: the search box is not an oracle, and ranking is not truth. "It was the top result" and "lots of pages say it" tell you about *optimization and coordination*, not about reality. Doing your own research means evaluating sources — not just typing a phrase someone fed you and trusting whatever appears.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Search Manipulation]
- **"Search this exact phrase."** A specific, unusual term you're told to look up is a red flag — you may be aimed at a data void.
- **Suspiciously unanimous results.** A top page full of agreement from unknown sites can be coordinated, not consensus.
- **Thin, fringe-only evidence.** A big claim that lives only in obscure corners may be filling a void on purpose.
- **Top ≠ true.** Ranking reflects optimization and popularity, not accuracy.

**VO:** One line to keep: *whoever chooses the words you search with has a head start on the answer you'll find.* Choose your own words, check your sources, and treat "do your own research" as a real skill — not a magic phrase.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, take one claim you've seen pushed with "just search [phrase]." Do it twice: first type their exact phrase, then research the topic in your own neutral words from credible sources.
