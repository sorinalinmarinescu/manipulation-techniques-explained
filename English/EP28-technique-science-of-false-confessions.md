## SEGMENT 1 — Steps eight and nine: building a "credible" confession

**VO:** Once you've cracked, steps eight and nine convert your admission into evidence. Step eight: you narrate the offense in detail. Step nine: it's written up and signed. Sounds neutral — but here's the catch that haunts false-confession cases.

**VO:** The details don't have to come from you. In a long interrogation, facts about the crime can leak — through leading questions, photos on the table, "isn't it true that...?" phrasing, or simply hours of the interrogator describing what they believe happened. An innocent person, with no real memory to draw on, can absorb those details and feed them back, now wrapped in the first person. The finished confession then contains "inside knowledge" that seems to prove guilt — when in fact the knowledge was handed to them in the room.

**VO:** That's why a false confession can read as devastatingly real. It's not a vague "okay, I did it." It's a narrative, with specifics, in the suspect's own words, signed at the bottom. It is the single most powerful piece of evidence a jury can hear — which is exactly what makes it so dangerous when it's false.

## SEGMENT 2 — The hard numbers

[GFX: a large stat builds: "~25%".]

**VO:** Let's put evidence on the table, because this is where I promised we wouldn't hedge.

**VO:** The Innocence Project tracks people convicted of crimes and later exonerated by DNA — cases where we now know, scientifically, that the person was innocent. In roughly **one in four** of those wrongful convictions, the innocent person had **confessed, admitted, or made incriminating statements** to police. Read that again: about 25 percent of people later *proven* innocent by DNA had, at some point, "confessed."

[LOWER THIRD: ~25% of DNA-exoneration wrongful convictions involved false confessions/admissions · Source: Innocence Project]

**VO:** An independent academic analysis of 194 DNA exonerations found false confessions in about **30 percent** of those cases. Different sample, same alarming order of magnitude. These aren't hypotheticals or one famous anecdote — they're patterns across hundreds of proven wrongful convictions.

[LOWER THIRD: False confessions in ~30% of 194 DNA-exoneration cases · Garrett (2011), peer-reviewed analysis]

**VO:** And remember, DNA exonerations are only the cases where biological evidence happened to exist and survive. They're the tip of an iceberg. We simply don't know how many false confessions never get caught because there was no DNA to test.

## SEGMENT 3 — Why juries believe them anyway

[GFX: a juror thinking, "Why would anyone confess to something they didn't do?"]

**VO:** Here's the trap that closes around the innocent. Almost everyone shares one powerful intuition: *I would never confess to something I didn't do, so no one else would either.* It feels like common sense. It's wrong — and you now know *why* it's wrong, because you've watched the machine that produces it across the last five episodes: the presumption of guilt, the broken lie-detection, the isolation, the crushed denials, the minimizing theme, the alternative-question trap, the leaked details.

**VO:** Because jurors trust that intuition, a confession often overwhelms everything else — even when other evidence points the other way, even when the confession contradicts the facts. Confession evidence is so persuasive that it can swamp contradictory DNA. That's the deadly combination: a technique that can manufacture confessions, feeding a justice system that treats confessions as nearly infallible.

## SEGMENT 4 — Who is most at risk

[GFX: icons — a teenager, a person with an intellectual disability, an exhausted person, a frightened first-timer.]

**VO:** False confessions aren't evenly distributed. Decades of research — notably a major scientific review by psychologist Saul Kassin and colleagues in 2010 — identify who breaks most easily under these tactics.

[LOWER THIRD: Risk factors — Kassin et al. (2010), "Police-Induced Confessions: Risk Factors and Recommendations"]

**VO:** **Young people.** Teenagers are dramatically over-represented in false-confession cases. They're more impulsive, more deferential to authority, and more likely to choose "make it stop now" over "protect myself later." **People with intellectual disabilities or mental-health conditions**, who may be more suggestible, more eager to please, or less able to grasp the consequences. And, frankly, **ordinary people pushed far enough** — the innocent and exhausted, who never imagined a confession could be coerced and so never armored themselves against it. Add lawful-but-powerful tactics — long isolation, lying about evidence, minimization that implies leniency — and the risk climbs.

**VO:** This is the moral core of the whole module: a technique built to overcome a guilty person's resistance applies the very same force to the innocent, the young, and the vulnerable — the people least able to withstand it.

## SEGMENT 5 — The reckoning, and the owner's defense (labeled)

[GFX: a timeline — "1962: manual published" → "2017: major trainer drops it" → "PEACE model spreads."]

**VO:** The field is moving. In 2017, Wicklander-Zulawski — a consulting firm that says it has trained a majority of U.S. police departments and had taught Reid-style methods since 1984 — announced it would **stop teaching the Reid technique**, citing the risk of false confessions. That's not a critic on the sidelines; that's one of the technique's biggest former vendors walking away.

[LOWER THIRD: Wicklander-Zulawski stops teaching the Reid technique (2017) · Sources: The Marshall Project; Innocence Project]

**VO:** Meanwhile, England and Wales abandoned confession-driven interrogation decades ago. Since the early 1990s their police have used the **PEACE model** — an information-gathering approach that aims at a full, accurate account rather than a confession, forbids deception, and starts from the presumption of innocence. Its principles are spreading worldwide and now inform international standards on lawful interviewing. The point: you *can* investigate effectively without psychologically cornering people. Confessions still happen — just not manufactured ones.

[LOWER THIRD: PEACE model (England & Wales, since ~1992) — non-accusatory, information-gathering]

**VO:** Now, in fairness and on the record: the company that owns the Reid technique, John E. Reid & Associates, **disputes** all of this. Their position is that their method, *properly applied*, is not coercive and does not cause false confessions — that the real culprit is improper use, like lying about evidence or implying leniency, which they say isn't true Reid. They've also begun offering their own PEACE-style program. You can weigh that for yourself. But weigh it knowing who's speaking: the party that profits from the technique defending the technique. That's their right — but it's a self-interested denial, not a neutral counterweight to a large body of independent evidence. Follow the evidence.

## SEGMENT 6 — How to spot it / why it matters to you

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Confession Evidence]
- **"They confessed" is not the end of the story.** Ask: under what conditions? How long? Recorded? Any chance the details were fed to them?
- **The intuition is the trap.** "No innocent person would confess" is exactly the false belief that convicts the innocent.
- **Watch for the vulnerable.** Be especially skeptical of confessions from juveniles or people with disabilities.
- **Recording matters.** A confession from an unrecorded, hours-long interrogation deserves far more scrutiny than the tidy signed page suggests.

**VO:** Why does this matter if you're never in that room? Because you sit on juries. You vote on justice policy. You read the news. Knowing that confessions can be manufactured changes how you weigh them — and that knowledge, multiplied across enough citizens, is part of what protects the next innocent person.

## SEGMENT 7 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, when you see a news story that leans on a confession, notice your own gut reaction — "well, he confessed, so..." — and then deliberately ask the questions from this episode. How long was the interrogation? Was it recorded start to finish? Was the suspect young or vulnerable? You're not deciding the case. You're practicing the habit of not letting the word "confession" switch off your judgment.
