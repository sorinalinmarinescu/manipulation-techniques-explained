# Episode 54 — Dark Patterns
### The interface tricks that deceive your clicks

**Module 7 · Episode 54** · **Target runtime:** 12–14 min
**Anchor callback:** EP11 (defaults & friction); EP53.
**Logline:** Dark patterns are interface designs crafted to trick you into doing things you didn't mean to — signing up, paying more, sharing data, or failing to cancel. This episode catalogs the common ones so you can recognize them on sight.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define dark patterns and learn the named varieties.
- Understand the psychology each one exploits.
- Build defensive habits for checkouts, sign-ups, and cancellations.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Dark pattern (deceptive design):** a user interface deliberately crafted to trick people into actions they didn't intend — buying, subscribing, sharing data, or failing to cancel.

**VO:** The term was coined in 2010 by a user-experience specialist named Harry Brignull, who got tired of watching the design profession's tricks be used against the people they were supposed to serve. Dark patterns flip the goal of good design: instead of helping you do what *you* want easily, they make what *the company* wants easy and what you want hard.

[LOWER THIRD: "Dark patterns" coined by Harry Brignull (2010)]

**VO:** They've become so common — and so harmful — that regulators in several countries now investigate and fine them. But the best protection is still your own eye, because there's always a new variation. So let's learn the family.

## SEGMENT 2 — The catalog

[GFX: each pattern appears as a labeled "wanted poster" card.]

**VO:** Here are the usual suspects.

**VO:** **Confirmshaming** — that guilt-trip decline button: "No thanks, I don't want to save money." It uses shame to push you toward yes.

**VO:** **The roach motel** — easy to get in, very hard to get out. One-click to subscribe; a maze of menus, phone calls, and "are you sure?" screens to cancel. (Friction, weaponized — Episode 11.)

**VO:** **Drip pricing / hidden costs** — a low price up front, with fees, "service charges," and add-ons drizzled in step by step until the final total is far higher. By the time you see it, you're committed (sunk cost — Episode 41).

**VO:** **Sneak-into-basket and pre-ticked boxes** — extras added to your cart by default, or consent boxes already checked, exploiting your inertia. You have to *notice and act* to opt out.

**VO:** **Forced continuity** — a "free trial" that silently converts to a paid subscription, betting you'll forget. Often paired with a roach-motel cancellation.

**VO:** **Misdirection and trick questions** — the design draws your eye to the choice they want; confusing double-negatives ("untick if you don't want to not receive emails") make you fumble into consent.

**VO:** **Nagging** — relentless pop-ups asking for permissions, reviews, or notifications until you say yes just to make it stop.

## SEGMENT 3 — Why they work on your brain

[GFX: a tired brain on autopilot tapping the bright button.]

**VO:** Dark patterns exploit predictable mental shortcuts. We're on **autopilot** — we don't read carefully; we tap the big bright button and assume defaults are sensible. We follow **visual hierarchy** — the eye goes where design leads it, so making one option loud and the other faint steers us without force. We feel **loss and shame** — confirmshaming and "you'll miss out" make declining emotionally costly. And we're **lazy about friction** — if cancelling is annoying enough, we put it off, maybe forever, which is exactly the bet.

**VO:** Crucially, they work *because* we're busy and trusting. We assume the company has arranged things reasonably and in good faith. Dark patterns betray that assumption on purpose, hiding the manipulation inside the ordinary, trusted flow of "just using the app."

## SEGMENT 4 — Defending your clicks

[GFX: a person slowing down at the checkout, reading the grey text.]

**VO:** The defense is to switch *off* autopilot at the high-risk moments: sign-ups, checkouts, and cancellations.

[ON SCREEN — THE DARK-PATTERN DEFENSE:]
- **Slow down at money and consent.** These are exactly where dark patterns live. Read the small grey text; it's small and grey on purpose.
- **Hunt the real "no."** Assume the genuine decline option is hidden or shamed. Look for it deliberately before clicking the bright button.
- **Check the cart and the total.** Before paying, scan for sneaked-in extras and watch the price *climb* through the steps (drip pricing).
- **Set a cancel reminder.** For any free trial, immediately note the charge date — and assume cancelling will be a roach motel, so start early.
- **Re-read double negatives.** When a checkbox is confusingly worded, slow down and translate it. They count on you fumbling.

**VO:** The mindset: at the moment of signing up, paying, or cancelling, treat the interface as an *adversary* until proven otherwise. Most of the time you'll just lose ten seconds. Occasionally you'll catch a charge, an add-on, or a data grab you never agreed to — and over a lifetime, that adds up to real money and real privacy.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Dark Patterns]
- **Guilt-trip decline.** A "no" option written to shame you = confirmshaming.
- **Easy in, hard out.** Effortless sign-up, nightmare cancellation = roach motel.
- **The climbing price.** Fees and extras appearing step by step = drip pricing.
- **Pre-checked / pre-added.** Defaults set in the company's favor, exploiting inertia.
- **The trick wording.** Confusing double negatives and misdirected buttons.

**VO:** The one-liner: *if declining is hard, shameful, or hidden, you're looking at a dark pattern — find the real "no."*

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Dark patterns are interfaces designed to trick your clicks — confirmshaming, roach motels, drip pricing, pre-ticked boxes, forced continuity, misdirection, nagging. They exploit autopilot and friction. The defense: slow down at sign-ups, checkouts, and cancellations, and always hunt for the real "no."
