# Episode 43 — Anchoring & the Decoy Effect
### How a planted number warps what you'll pay

**Module 5 · Episode 43** · **Target runtime:** 12–14 min
**Anchor callback:** EP38 — the overpriced "premium" phone that made the next one look reasonable.
**Logline:** We don't judge value in absolute terms; we judge by comparison. Manipulators exploit this with *anchoring* (planting a first number that drags your estimate toward it) and the *decoy effect* (adding a deliberately bad option to make another look great).

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand anchoring and why the first number sticks.
- Understand the decoy effect / asymmetric dominance with a famous real example.
- Defend by setting your own reference points.

You're really just comparing to whatever number they showed you first.

## SEGMENT 1 — Anchoring: the first number wins

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Anchoring:** the tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information (often a number) when making judgments, so later estimates are pulled toward that "anchor."

**VO:** Our minds are bad at absolute values but good at comparisons — so we grab whatever reference point is handy, and the first number offered becomes that anchor. Everything after is judged relative to it. Show a high "original" price, and the sale price feels generous. Name a high salary first in a negotiation, and the final figure lands higher. The anchor doesn't even have to be relevant — research shows people's estimates get dragged toward numbers that are obviously random. That's how automatic this is.

**VO:** This connects to framing (Episode 9): the anchor sets the frame for "expensive" versus "cheap." Whoever puts the first number on the table has quietly drawn the map everyone then navigates.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — The decoy effect: the option that's there to lose

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Decoy effect (asymmetric dominance):** adding a third, deliberately inferior option that isn't meant to be chosen, but makes one of the original options look much better by comparison.

**VO:** Here's the famous demonstration, popularized by behavioral economist Dan Ariely from a real magazine pricing experiment. Imagine a subscription with two options: web-only for 59 dollars, or web-plus-print for 125. Offered just those two, most people pick the cheap web-only.

**VO:** Now add a third option: print-only, also for 125 dollars — the exact same price as web-plus-print, but clearly worse. Nobody in their right mind picks print-only; it's strictly dominated. So why include it? Because its mere presence makes web-plus-print look like an incredible deal ("same price, but I get *both*!"). In Ariely's telling, adding that useless decoy flipped the majority toward the expensive option. The decoy was never meant to be bought. It was there to make its neighbor shine.

[LOWER THIRD: Decoy effect — Huber, Payne & Puto (1982); popularized via Dan Ariely's Economist pricing example]

## SEGMENT 3 — Why these work on your brain

[GFX: three price tiers — a "useless" middle/extreme tier nudging the eye to the target.]

**VO:** Both tricks exploit the same truth: we evaluate by **relative comparison**, not absolute worth, because comparison is easier for our brains than figuring out what something is "really" worth. Manipulators simply supply the comparisons — choosing the anchor and the decoy so that the option *they* want looks best.

**VO:** That's why pricing menus so often come in three tiers, with a middle or top option engineered as a decoy to steer you to the "target." It's why the first offer in any negotiation matters enormously. It's why "compare at" and "manufacturer's suggested price" exist — to plant a high anchor so the actual price feels like a win. None of it changes the thing's real value. It changes the *yardstick* you measure it with.

## SEGMENT 4 — Setting your own yardstick

[GFX: a person bringing their own ruler instead of using the store's.]

**VO:** The defense is to bring your *own* reference points, decided before they show you theirs.

[ON SCREEN — THE NUMBER DEFENSE:]
- **Anchor yourself first.** Before shopping or negotiating, decide independently what the thing is worth *to you* and what you'll pay. Now their first number can't set your map.
- **Ignore "was" prices.** The "original" or "compare at" price is an anchor, often fictional. Judge the actual price against real alternatives, not against their inflated reference.
- **Spot the decoy.** In any 3-option menu, ask which option seems *designed to be rejected* — and which one it's pushing you toward. Then ask if that target is actually what *you* need.
- **Compare outside their frame.** Check independent prices and options elsewhere, so your comparison set isn't the one they curated.

**VO:** The core move: refuse to let them choose your comparison. The moment you measure against *your* needs and *outside* prices — not the anchor and decoy they planted — the spell breaks, and a "great deal" is just a price again.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Anchoring & Decoys]
- **A loud first number.** "Was $999," "valued at," a high opening offer — that's an anchor, not a fact.
- **The three-tier menu.** Look for the option that exists to be rejected, steering you to the one beside it.
- **"Great by comparison."** If something only looks good *next to* what they showed you, change what you compare it to.
- **Bring your own number.** Did you decide your price before they spoke, or did they decide it for you?

**VO:** The one-liner: *value is relative, so whoever picks the comparison picks the winner — make sure it's you.*

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, before any purchase over a certain amount, write down — privately, first — what you think it's worth and your max price. *Then* look at their pricing. Notice the anchors ("was" prices) and the decoy tiers, and see how hard they tug you off your own number. Holding your pre-decided figure is one of the most quietly powerful money skills there is.

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** We judge by comparison, so anchoring plants a first number that drags your estimate, and the decoy effect adds a junk option to make another shine. The defense is to set your own yardstick first and compare outside their frame — because whoever picks the comparison picks the winner.
