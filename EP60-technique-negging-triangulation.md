# Episode 60 — Negging & Triangulation
### Undermining you, and playing people against each other

**Module 8 · Episode 60** · **Target runtime:** 12–13 min
**Anchor callback:** EP56's "control" stage; both tactics manufacture insecurity to gain power.
**Logline:** Two control tactics that work by destabilizing you. Negging is the backhanded compliment or subtle put-down that chips away at your confidence so you seek the manipulator's approval. Triangulation brings in a third person — a rival, an ally, an audience — to control you through comparison, jealousy, and divide-and-rule.

**Learning objectives:**
- Recognize negging and why lowered self-esteem increases compliance.
- Recognize triangulation and divide-and-conquer dynamics.
- Restore your footing through self-worth and direct communication.

## SEGMENT 1 — Negging: the put-down disguised as a compliment

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Negging:** a backhanded compliment or subtle criticism designed to undermine someone's confidence, making them seek the manipulator's approval.

**VO:** Negging — from "negative" — is a deliberate small put-down wrapped in a compliment's clothing. It first got public attention as a sleazy pickup tactic, but it's used far more widely, by controlling partners, "frenemies," and manipulative bosses. The logic is dark but simple: if I can knock your confidence down a notch, you'll be less secure, more eager for my approval, and easier to influence.

**VO:** It works through that ambiguous, plausibly-deniable form. A straight insult would make you angry and push you away. But a *compliment with a barb* leaves you off-balance — was that mean, or am I being too sensitive? You can't cleanly object ("it was a compliment!"), so the doubt sits and works on you. Over time, repeated negs erode self-esteem, and a person with shaky self-esteem is far more compliant, more grateful for crumbs of approval, and less likely to leave.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Triangulation: the manufactured third party

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Triangulation:** introducing a third person into a relationship dynamic to control, compare, divide, or destabilize.

**VO:** Triangulation brings a third point into what should be a two-person matter. Instead of dealing with you directly, the manipulator routes things *through* someone else — to create jealousy, competition, doubt, or division.

**VO:** It takes several forms. **Comparison and jealousy:** "My ex never complained about this." "Everyone else thinks you're overreacting." Holding up a rival — real or invented — so you feel you must compete for your place. **Divide and conquer:** in families, friend groups, or teams, the manipulator tells different people different things, sets them against each other, and positions themselves as the central hub everyone depends on — because people busy distrusting each other won't unite against the manipulator. **Recruiting allies:** dragging in "everyone agrees with me" or a flying-monkey third party to gang up, so you feel outnumbered and isolated.

## SEGMENT 3 — Why they work on your brain

[GFX: a self-esteem meter being lowered (negging) and a person triangulated between two others (triangulation).]

**VO:** Both tactics share one goal: **make you insecure, because insecure people are easier to control.**

**VO:** Negging exploits the fact that our self-image is partly built from how others treat us — so a steady drip of subtle put-downs genuinely lowers how we see ourselves, especially from someone we care about. And lowered self-worth creates a craving for approval — ideally from the very person who lowered it, which hands them enormous power. It's a closed loop: wound the confidence, then become the source of the cure.

**VO:** Triangulation exploits our deep sensitivity to **status, belonging, and rivalry** (the in-group instincts from Episode 16). Jealousy and competition are powerful, primitive feelings; manufacturing them keeps you anxious and striving. And divide-and-conquer exploits the simple truth that isolated, mutually-suspicious people can't support each other or compare notes — which, as we've seen all module, is exactly the condition manipulation needs.

## SEGMENT 4 — Restoring your footing

[GFX: a person standing steady, valuing themselves, talking directly to each party.]

**VO:** The defenses target what each tactic attacks: your self-worth, and your direct connections to others.

[ON SCREEN — THE DEFENSE:]
- **Name the neg.** When a "compliment" stings, don't dismiss your reaction. Say (even just to yourself): "That was a put-down dressed as a compliment." You can also calmly call it out: "That didn't sound like a compliment." Manipulators rely on you staying silent to keep deniability.
- **Anchor your self-worth outside them.** The more your sense of value depends on one person's approval, the more power negging has. Build esteem from many sources — your own standards, other relationships, your accomplishments.
- **Go direct.** Triangulation lives in indirect, routed-through-others communication. Defeat it by talking *directly* to the person involved: "Let's you and I sort this out, rather than through other people."
- **Compare notes.** Divide-and-conquer dies when the divided people *talk to each other.* If you suspect someone is telling different people different stories, the antidote is for those people to compare directly.
- **Refuse the competition.** You don't have to compete for your place against a manufactured rival. A relationship that requires you to constantly out-compete someone for basic respect isn't a prize worth winning.

**VO:** The core principle: both tactics try to make your sense of worth and security *dependent on the manipulator*. The defense is to root your self-worth and your relationships *outside* their control — so they have nothing to lower and no triangle to trap you in.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Negging & Triangulation]
- **Compliments that sting.** Praise that leaves you feeling worse or doubting yourself = negging.
- **Always being compared.** To an ex, a sibling, a colleague, "everyone else" — manufactured rivalry.
- **Routed communication.** Important things reach you *through* third parties instead of directly.
- **Everyone's at odds, one person's at the center.** Divide-and-conquer with the manipulator as the hub.

**VO:** The one-liner: *both tactics shrink you and isolate you — so anchor your worth outside them and talk to people directly.*

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, two small practices. First, if you get a "compliment" that stings, don't brush off your reaction — quietly label it, and notice who in your life does this regularly. Second, if you ever hear "so-and-so said this about you," resist the triangle: go to the source and check directly. Direct communication is kryptonite to triangulation, and you'll be amazed how often the "rivalry" or "conflict" simply evaporates.

**VO:** Negging chips at your confidence with put-downs disguised as compliments; triangulation uses a third party to create jealousy and division. Both manufacture insecurity to make you easier to control. The defense: anchor your self-worth outside the manipulator, name the neg, and communicate directly to dissolve the triangle.
