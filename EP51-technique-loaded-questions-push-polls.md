# Episode 51 — Loaded Questions & Push Polls
### Questions rigged so any answer hurts you

**Module 6 · Episode 51** · **Target runtime:** 11–13 min
**Anchor callback:** EP47's crooked debate; the question form of framing (EP9).
**Logline:** A loaded question smuggles an assumption inside it, so that any direct answer traps you. A push poll disguises an attack as a survey. Both weaponize the *question* — because a question can plant a claim while pretending only to ask.

**Learning objectives:**
- Recognize the hidden assumption inside a loaded question.
- Understand push polls as attacks disguised as research.
- Refuse the trap by rejecting the premise, not answering it.

There's no honest answer, because the trap was built into the question. You were never being asked something; you were being *accused*, in the shape of a question.

## SEGMENT 1 — The loaded question

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Loaded question:** a question containing a built-in assumption, so that any direct answer forces you to accept that assumption.

**VO:** The classic example — "have you stopped beating your spouse?" — shows the mechanism perfectly. The question presupposes you *were* beating your spouse. Both "yes" and "no" concede the presupposition. The question isn't seeking information; it's smuggling a claim past your defenses by hiding it inside something that looks like a neutral inquiry.

**VO:** This is framing (Episode 9) in question form. A statement can be challenged: if I assert "you wasted the money," you can argue. But a *question* — "why did you waste the money?" — sneaks the same accusation in while putting *you* on the defensive, scrambling to answer rather than to dispute. The assumption rides in under the radar, because we're trained to answer questions, not interrogate them.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a question mark with a hidden claim smuggled inside it, slipping past a "guard."]

**VO:** Loaded questions work because of a conversational reflex: when someone asks us a direct question, we feel obligated to *answer* it, not to challenge its premise. Refusing to answer feels evasive, even rude — which is exactly the discomfort the trap exploits. "Why won't you just give a straight answer?" they press, when the honest move is to reject the question itself.

**VO:** They also exploit the audience. In a debate or interview, a loaded question plants its assumption in *everyone's* mind, whether or not you answer. Even "I reject the premise" leaves the accusation hanging in the air — the listeners heard "taxes" and "cheating" in the same breath. The question did its damage just by being asked. That's why they're so beloved by hostile interviewers and attack campaigns.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — The push poll: an attack wearing a lab coat

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Push poll:** political telemarketing disguised as an opinion survey, designed to spread negative information and shift voters, not to measure opinion.

**VO:** Now the industrial-scale version. A push poll *pretends* to be a survey — "we're conducting a poll, do you have a moment?" — but it isn't measuring anything. Its real purpose is to *push* a message into your head under the disguise of research. The questions are loaded by design: "Would you be more or less likely to vote for Candidate X if you knew they [insert damaging claim, often false]?"

**VO:** Notice the trick. They're not claiming the smear is true — they're "just asking" whether it would affect your vote. But the question plants the smear regardless. You've now heard the damaging idea, dressed in the authority of a neutral-sounding poll. Professional polling organizations condemn push polls precisely because they're propaganda masquerading as research — they borrow the credibility of real surveys (Episode 3's borrowed authority) to launder an attack.

[LOWER THIRD: Push polls — condemned by AAPOR as "political telemarketing disguised as a poll"]

## SEGMENT 4 — Rejecting the premise

[GFX: a person calmly stepping around the trap instead of into it.]

**VO:** The defense for both is the same and it's liberating: **don't answer a rigged question — challenge it.**

[ON SCREEN — THE LOADED-QUESTION DEFENSE:]
- **Name the hidden assumption.** "That question assumes I did X, which isn't true. Let's start there." You're allowed to reject the premise instead of answering.
- **Refuse the false yes/no.** "I won't accept the framing of that question" is a complete, legitimate response. Don't let "just answer" bully you into the trap.
- **For 'polls': check if it's real.** A genuine survey measures opinion and doesn't feed you damaging "what if you knew..." claims. If it's pushing a message, it's a push poll — hang up.
- **Spot 'just asking questions.'** When someone plants an ugly claim by "merely asking," call it out: "You're not asking, you're implying. Say it plainly or drop it."

**VO:** The mindset shift: a question is not automatically innocent, and you are not obligated to answer it on its own terms. The most powerful move against a loaded question is to refuse the cage entirely — step back, expose the assumption, and answer the question you *should* have been asked.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Loaded Questions & Push Polls]
- **No honest answer fits.** If every direct answer makes you concede something false, the question is loaded.
- **A claim hidden in a question.** "Why did you [assumed bad thing]?" smuggles the accusation in.
- **"Just asking questions."** A smear delivered as a question is still a smear.
- **A 'survey' that pushes.** "Would you vote differently if you knew [damaging claim]?" = push poll, not research.

**VO:** The one-liner: *you don't have to answer a rigged question — you can reject it.* Challenge the premise, and the trap springs harmlessly shut.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, practice one phrase until it's comfortable: "I don't accept the premise of that question." Try it on a leading question in a real conversation (gently!). And if you ever get a "poll" call that starts feeding you negative claims about a candidate, recognize it instantly as a push poll and hang up. Reclaiming your right to refuse a question is a surprisingly powerful feeling.
